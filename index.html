Iris Labs Deep Sky Images

IC 1318 – The Gamma Cygni Nebula

IC 1318 is a vast emission nebula complex in the constellation Cygnus, centered around the bright star Gamma Cygni (Sadr). This region features intricate filaments of ionized hydrogen glowing in H-alpha light, interspersed with dark dust lanes that create a striking contrast often referred to as the Butterfly Nebula in wide-field views. As part of the Cygnus OB association, it represents an active star-forming region where massive stars energize the surrounding gas.

The nebula lies approximately 5,000 light-years from Earth. The photons captured in this image departed the nebula around 3,000 BCE, during the early Bronze Age. At that time on Earth, the first urban civilizations were emerging in Mesopotamia and the Indus Valley, while monumental architecture such as the early Egyptian pyramids was under construction.

IC 727 – Spiral Galaxy in Leo

IC 727 is a faint spiral galaxy in the constellation Leo, often imaged alongside its brighter companion NGC 672, with which it appears to interact tidally. The galaxy exhibits loosely wound arms and a modest central bulge, typical of intermediate-type spirals influenced by gravitational encounters.

IC 727 is located roughly 35 million light-years away. The light we see today left the galaxy during the early Oligocene epoch, approximately 35 million years ago. On Earth, this period marked significant global cooling, the expansion of grasslands, and the diversification of early mammals following the Eocene–Oligocene extinction event.

Messier 101 – The Pinwheel Galaxy

Messier 101, commonly known as the Pinwheel Galaxy, is a face-on grand-design spiral galaxy in Ursa Major. Its asymmetric appearance and prominent star-forming regions result from past gravitational interactions with companion galaxies, producing vivid H II regions and young star clusters visible along its sweeping arms.

At a distance of about 21 million light-years, the photons in this image began their journey during the early Miocene epoch. Around 21 million years ago on Earth, the ancestors of modern great apes were diverging, extensive grasslands were spreading across continents, and early members of the horse and elephant lineages were evolving.

Messier 106

Messier 106 is a nearby spiral galaxy in Canes Venatici with anomalous arms visible in radio and X-ray wavelengths, powered by an active galactic nucleus. Its optical appearance reveals tightly wound arms and a bright core, with evidence of recent star formation influenced by the central supermassive black hole.

Situated approximately 24 million light-years away, the light captured here originated in the early Miocene. During this time on Earth, tectonic activity was reshaping continents, early apes were appearing in Africa, and the modern configuration of ocean currents was beginning to form.

Messier 13 (Hercules Globular Cluster) and IC 4617

Messier 13 is one of the brightest globular clusters in the northern sky, containing several hundred thousand stars in a dense spherical distribution within the Milky Way's halo. This image also captures the faint background spiral galaxy IC 4617, visible near the cluster—a distant extragalactic object unrelated to M13.

M13 lies about 22,000 light-years away, so its light departed during the Last Glacial Maximum, when vast ice sheets covered much of the northern hemisphere and early modern humans were creating cave art in Europe. In contrast, IC 4617 is approximately 480 million light-years distant; its photons left during the Cambrian period, when complex multicellular life was rapidly diversifying in Earth's oceans.

Messier 51 – The Whirlpool Galaxy

Messier 51, the Whirlpool Galaxy, is an interacting grand-design spiral in Canes Venatici, connected by a tidal bridge to its companion NGC 5195. The interaction has triggered intense star formation, producing bright knots and dust lanes that define its classic whorled appearance.

Located roughly 25 million light-years away, the light we observe today left during the late Oligocene to early Miocene transition. On Earth, this era saw the rise of early apes, the spread of savannas, and significant mammalian radiations following major climatic shifts.

Messier 57 (The Ring Nebula) and IC 1296

Messier 57, the Ring Nebula, is a classic planetary nebula in Lyra—the ejected outer envelope of a dying sun-like star, illuminated by its hot central white dwarf. This image also includes the distant barred spiral galaxy IC 1296, appearing nearby in the field but lying far beyond the nebula.

M57 is approximately 2,500 light-years distant, meaning its light left around 500 BCE, during the height of classical antiquity when Greek philosophers were laying the foundations of Western science. IC 1296, at about 220 million light-years, emitted its light during the late Triassic period, when the first dinosaurs and early mammals were emerging on Earth.

Messier 63 – The Sunflower Galaxy

Messier 63, nicknamed the Sunflower Galaxy, is a flocculent spiral in Canes Venatici with patchy, fragmented arms rich in star-forming regions. Its multi-armed structure lacks the grand-design symmetry of some spirals, reflecting a more chaotic distribution of density waves.

At a distance of approximately 29 million light-years, the photons originated in the early Miocene. During this period on Earth, major tectonic collisions were forming the Himalayas, early hominoids were diversifying, and global climates were cooling toward modern patterns.

NGC 6339 – Barred Spiral Galaxy in Hercules

NGC 6339 is a loosely wound barred spiral galaxy in Hercules, characterized by a prominent central bar and diffuse arms. Its low surface brightness makes it a challenging target, revealing subtle structure only in deep exposures.

Situated around 100 million light-years away, the light we see departed during the late Cretaceous period. At that time on Earth, dinosaurs dominated the land, flowering plants were proliferating, and the Atlantic Ocean was widening as continents drifted apart.

NGC 6888 – The Crescent Nebula

NGC 6888, the Crescent Nebula, is a wind-blown emission nebula in Cygnus energized by the massive Wolf-Rayet star WR 136. The characteristic crescent shape results from the star's powerful stellar winds colliding with previously ejected material.

Approximately 5,000 light-years distant, the light left around 3,000 BCE. This corresponds to the early Bronze Age on Earth, when writing systems were developing in Sumer and Egypt, marking the transition from prehistory to recorded history.

NGC 7331 – The Deer Lick Galaxy

NGC 7331 is a large unbarred spiral galaxy in Pegasus, often considered a twin to the Milky Way in structure. Its inclined disk reveals prominent dust lanes and a bright bulge, while nearby faint companions form the Deer Lick Group.

At about 40 million light-years, the photons originated in the Eocene epoch. Around 40 million years ago on Earth, early primates were thriving in forested environments, and the India-Asia collision was initiating the uplift of the Tibetan Plateau.

NGC 7635 – The Bubble Nebula

NGC 7635, the Bubble Nebula, is an H II region in Cassiopeia created by the stellar wind of the massive central star SAO 20575. The symmetric bubble structure results from the wind sweeping up surrounding gas into a glowing shell.

Located roughly 8,000 light-years away, the light departed around 6,000 BCE, during the late Neolithic period. On Earth, agriculture was spreading across Europe and Asia, and the first permanent settlements were transitioning into early complex societies.

NGC 891 – Edge-On Spiral Galaxy

NGC 891 is a classic edge-on spiral galaxy in Andromeda, displaying a thin disk bisected by a prominent dust lane. Its structure offers a perspective similar to how the Milky Way might appear from outside, with a central bulge and extended warp.

At approximately 30 million light-years, the light left during the early Oligocene. This epoch on Earth saw dramatic cooling, the formation of Antarctic ice sheets, and the emergence of many modern mammalian orders.

NGC 925 – Barred Spiral Galaxy

NGC 925 is a barred spiral galaxy in Triangulum with open, flocculent arms and numerous star-forming regions. As a member of the NGC 1023 group, it exhibits asymmetric features possibly influenced by minor interactions.

Situated about 30 million light-years away, the photons originated in the Oligocene epoch. During this time on Earth, extensive forests were giving way to open habitats, and early ancestors of elephants, horses, and cats were diversifying.

Sh2-101 – The Tulip Nebula

Sh2-101, commonly known as the Tulip Nebula, is a bright emission nebula in Cygnus excited by the young star cluster within it. Its blooming flower-like appearance arises from dense gas clouds sculpted by radiation and winds from hot O-type stars.

Approximately 6,000 light-years distant, the light captured here left around 4,000 BCE, during the late Neolithic to early Chalcolithic period. On Earth, megalithic structures were being erected in Europe, and the first proto-writing systems were emerging in the Near East.
